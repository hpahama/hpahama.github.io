Title: We Need to Fix Black Hair in Video Games

Introduction

The representation of Black hair in video games is a critical aspect of promoting diversity and inclusivity. As players, we engage with virtual worlds that often mirror our own experiences and perceptions. In this paper, we explore the significance of authentic hair representation, the challenges faced by game developers, and the impact such portrayals can have on players’ perspectives.

Authenticity Matters: The Journey of Isaac Olander

Growing up in Gambia, West Africa, Isaac Olander, CEO and art director at Swedish-based studio Tallgran, encountered a lack of representation in Western and Swedish media. However, Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas stood out as a game that broke barriers. It allowed customization, including three distinct hairstyles for the Black protagonist, CJ: buzzcut, afro, and cornrows. For Olander, this was a pivotal moment—the first time he felt truly represented, where his natural hair was normalized. Despite the game’s now-dated graphics, GTA: San Andreas pioneered accurate human character representation, leaving a lasting impact on players like Olander.

Code My Crown: A Step Forward

Recognizing the ongoing challenge of representing Black hair in games, Dove partnered with the Open Source Afro Hair Library to create Code My Crown. This groundbreaking initiative provides a complete and free guide for coding textured hair and protective styles in video games. By empowering coders and developers, this resource aims to enhance the authenticity of Black hairstyles in 3D environments. It’s a step toward more accurate and respectful portrayals, bridging the gap between virtual representation and real-world diversity.

Impact and Perception

Video games have a unique ability to shape our minds and foster acceptance of diversity. When games accurately depict various hair types, they challenge stereotypes and broaden players’ perspectives. Even curlier hair, regardless of ethnicity, presents design challenges. Developers must consider factors like physics, animation, and cultural context to create authentic representations. By doing so, games can normalize diverse hair textures and contribute to a more inclusive gaming experience.

Conclusion

As players, developers, and advocates, we must continue pushing for authentic representation in video games. Let’s celebrate the strides made by initiatives like Code My Crown and encourage more studios to prioritize diversity. By doing so, we create virtual worlds where everyone feels seen, valued, and empowered—one textured hair at a time.
